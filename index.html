About Me

My name is Samira Vafay Eslahi, and I will be joining the University of North Florida (UNF) in Fall 2026 as a Tenure-Track Assistant Professor in Electrical Engineering.

I received my Ph.D. in Electrical Engineering from Texas A&M University in 2022. My dissertation, entitled "Convolutional Neural Network Optimization and Parallel Compressive Sensing Algorithms for Accelerated MRI Reconstruction", was supervised by Professor Jim Ji (Chair) and Dr. Jian Tao (Co-Chair).

After my Ph.D., I joined Gordon Center for Medical Imaging at Harvard Medical School and Massachusetts General Hospital as a T32 postdoctoral research fellow, where I conducted research, contributed to MRI data acquisition, and developed computational tools in medical image processing for two years (2022–2024) under the supervision of Dr. Jinsong Ouyang (Mentor), Dr. Chao Ma (Mentor), Professor Georges El Fakhri (T32 Director), and Professor Ciprian Catana (T32 Director).

Following my postdoctoral fellowship, in 2025 I joined Southern Illinois University Carbondale (SIUC), School of Electrical, Computer and Biomedical Engineering, as an Assistant Professor of Practice in Electrical Engineering. At SIUC, I taught Graduate and Undergraduate courses in Digital Image Processing, Signal Processing and Analysis, and Programming. I also served as a member of the Electrical Engineering (EE) Program Committee, contributing to EE program development.

Research Directions

1. Magnetic Resonance Image (MRI) Reconstruction

We are developing robust and reliable artificial intelligence (AI)-based techniques for MR image reconstruction to reduce scan time and computational cost while enabling more flexible imaging protocols.

2. Medical Image Processing for Alzheimer's Disease Diagnosis and Monitoring

Our research develops image processing and artificial intelligence (AI) methods to improve medical imaging for the diagnosis and monitoring of Alzheimer's disease.

Positron Emission Tomography (PET) Denoising

We develop deep learning methods to denoise low-count PET images, improving image quality while reducing scan time and radiation exposure. Our work focuses on robust and generalizable AI models that preserve clinically relevant information for reliable diagnosis and quantitative analysis.

MRI Quantification

We develop quantitative MRI methods that estimate tissue parameters directly from MRI data, enabling the extraction of biomarkers and imaging features that are not readily visible in conventional reconstructed images.

  • Quantitative Susceptibility Mapping (QSM): Quantifies magnetic susceptibility to assess iron deposition.
  • T1 and T2 Mapping: Measures tissue relaxation times to characterize tissue microstructure.
  • B1 Mapping: Estimates radiofrequency (RF) field inhomogeneity.

Our group develops quantitative imaging methods to estimate biomarkers associated with Alzheimer's disease directly from MRI. In particular, we investigate techniques for predicting the accumulation of amyloid-beta (Aβ) (a protein that abnormally accumulates in the brains of individuals with Alzheimer's disease) from noninvasive MRI, with the goal of enabling earlier diagnosis before brain changes structural changes.
